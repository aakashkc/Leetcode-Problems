You want:

if loop executes at least once AND finishes normally → return one value
if loop never runs → return another value
Best Way

Use a flag variable.

def test(x):

    loop_ran = False

    while x > 0:

        loop_ran = True
        x -= 1

    if loop_ran:
        return "Loop finished"
    else:
        return "Loop never ran"
Example 1
test(3)

Output:

Loop finished

because loop executed.

Example 2
test(0)

Output:

Loop never ran

because condition was False initially.

What is Digital Root?

The digital root means:

Keep adding digits until only one digit remains.

Example:

38 → 3 + 8 = 11 → 1 + 1 = 2

So digital root of 38 = 2

Why the formula works

digital root=1+(n−1)mod9

This formula is based on modulo 9 behavior of numbers.

Key Idea (Very Important)

Every number has a special property:

When you take:

sum of digits repeatedly

it behaves like:

n mod 9
But why mod 9?

Because:

10 ≡ 1 (mod 9)

That means:

10 behaves like 1 in modulo 9
100 behaves like 1
1000 behaves like 1

So place value disappears in mod 9.

Example

Take:

123
Step 1: normal sum
1 + 2 + 3 = 6
Step 2: mod 9 check
123 % 9 = 6

Same result.

Now the formula part

We use:

1+(n−1)mod9

instead of just:

n % 9
Why not just n % 9?

Because of a special case:

Problem with 9
9 % 9 = 0 ❌

But digital root of 9 should be:

9

So we fix it using:

1 + (n - 1) % 9
Let’s test it
Example 1
n = 38

Step:

1 + (38 - 1) % 9
= 1 + 37 % 9
= 1 + 1
= 2

✔ correct

Example 2
n = 9

Step:

1 + (9 - 1) % 9
= 1 + 8 % 9
= 1 + 8
= 9

✔ correct

Example 3
n = 18
1 + (18 - 1) % 9
= 1 + 17 % 9
= 1 + 8
= 9

✔ correct

Intuition in Simple Words

Think of numbers in a cycle of 9:

1 → 2 → 3 → ... → 9 → 1 → 2 → 3 ...

So:

numbers “wrap around” every 9
digital root is just position in this cycle
Super Simple Summary
Digital root = repeated digit sum
Same as modulo 9 pattern
Formula fixes the edge case of multiples of 9
