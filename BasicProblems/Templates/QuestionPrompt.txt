The Core Principle
Give GPT context + your code + specific questions. The more you narrow it down, the more useful the answer.

The Prompt Template You Should Use
Problem: [Problem name or description]
My approach: [e.g., "I used a sliding window with a hashmap"]
My code:
[paste your code]

Now tell me:
1. Is my time and space complexity correct? What is it actually?
2. What mistakes or bad habits do you see in my code?
3. Is there a cleaner or more optimal approach?
4. What edge cases did I miss that could break my solution?
5. How would a senior engineer write this differently?

Level-Up Variations
When you want to think deeper before seeing the answer:

"Don't give me the solution yet. Just tell me if my approach is on the right track and give me one hint."

When you solved it but feel it's messy:

"My solution works but feels brute-force. What pattern or data structure would make this more elegant? Explain why before showing code."

When you want to understand your own mistake:

"I got a wrong answer on this test case: [input] → expected [x] but got [y]. Walk me through why my logic fails here step by step."

When you want to think like an interviewer:

"If I gave this solution in a Google interview, what follow-up questions would the interviewer ask me? What would make them push back?"

When you want pattern recognition:

"What DSA pattern does this problem belong to? What are 3 other problems that use the exact same pattern so I can practice them?"


What Most People Get Wrong
❌ Weak ask✅ Strong ask"Is my code correct?""What's the time complexity and where exactly does it break down?""Show me a better solution""Show me a better solution and explain why mine is worse""Refactor this""Refactor this and call out every bad habit you see"Paste code onlyPaste code + your thought process

One Golden Rule
Always write your thought process before pasting code. Even one line like:

"I thought this was a two-pointer problem because the array is sorted..."

This forces GPT to evaluate your thinking, not just your syntax — which is where the real learning happens.
